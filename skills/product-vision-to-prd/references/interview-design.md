# Adaptive Vision Interview

Use only for consequential vision gaps. Reduce uncertainty per unit of user
effort rather than maximizing coverage.

## Build a gap map

Privately map what is known, inferred, contradictory, or missing:

- intended change and exceptional success;
- primary users, buyers, operators, maintainers, and affected non-users;
- the fundamentally broken status quo;
- the distinctive or magical experience;
- the role of Codex, Codex Sites, skills, or recurring loops;
- principles, boundaries, fears, commitments, and non-goals.

Ask only where the answer could materially alter product identity or direction.
Do not ask about every listed dimension.

## Design the batch

Present three to six named themes with one primary question each. Add cues only
to clarify the answer space; merge overlap and order themes coherently. First
synthesize current understanding and say the user may answer naturally,
incompletely, or out of order without addressing each cue.

- **Change:** If this worked exceptionally well, what would become meaningfully
  different for people?
- **People:** Who most needs that change, and who must operate, adopt, buy, or
  sustain the product?
- **Broken status quo:** What feels fundamentally wrong today rather than merely
  inconvenient?
- **Distinctive experience:** What should feel surprisingly better, newly
  possible, or unmistakably yours?
- **Boundaries and ambition:** What must the product protect, avoid becoming,
  and eventually make possible?

Adapt or omit these prompts; never copy them mechanically.

## Avoid

- Feature, screen, data-model, lifecycle, or MVP design.
- Repetitive questions or unrelated prompts hidden in one item.
- Confirmation when a labeled assumption is safe.
- Known information or rigid response formats.

## Pass conditions

Every question can change identity or direction; one natural message can answer
the batch; questions do not substantially overlap; routine decisions remain
delegated; and a useful incomplete answer still enables synthesis.
