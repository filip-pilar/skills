# Digital Technology Sector — Qualifying Guide

## Core Principle

Tech Nation endorses people working in the **digital technology sector**. The key question: is the applicant building, designing, leading, or innovating in digital technology — or are they using technology as a tool in another industry?

## Clearly Qualifying Roles

Technical roles (software engineering, ML/AI, data science, cybersecurity, DevOps, cloud, mobile, game dev, blockchain), product and design roles (PM, UX/UI, CTO, VP Engineering), and leadership/business roles at product-led tech companies (any C-suite/VP, founders) all clearly qualify. The key question is always the **company type**, not the role title — a CMO at a language learning app qualifies; a CMO at a non-tech company doesn't.

Emerging/specialized roles (AI safety, quantum computing, AR/VR, robotics, technical content creation) also qualify. If someone's profile is more creative than pure tech, mention the separate creator pathway.

## Roles That Need Positioning

These roles don't automatically qualify — but many can work with the right company context or supporting evidence. The key question is always **what company** and **what did you actually do**, not the job title.

### IT Services & Outsourcing
The outsourcing/consulting work itself is the hardest to position (see Borderline Cases below). But people at these firms can still qualify through work outside the role — open source, side projects, community contributions. Don't dismiss someone based on employer alone.

- IT outsourcing company employees → explore work outside the role
- Body shop / staff augmentation consultants → explore product work or side projects
- General IT consulting (Accenture-style) → borderline; look for product innovation, not just delivery
- IT support / helpdesk → unlikely unless at a tech company with innovative infra work
- System administration → can qualify at a tech company doing innovative infrastructure

### Adjacent Roles — Company Context Matters

**The company is the deciding factor.** These roles qualify at product-led digital technology companies but not at non-tech companies. A business analyst at a SaaS startup is in a very different position from a business analyst at a bank.

- Pure hardware engineering → look for software components or digital product integration
- Traditional project management (PMP-style) → can qualify if managing digital product delivery at a tech company
- Business analyst → qualifies at product-led tech companies
- Digital marketing / SEO → marketing leaders at product-led tech companies qualify; at non-tech companies, look for other angles
- Graphic design → qualifies if doing UX/UI for digital products, or at a product-led tech company
- IT procurement / vendor management → tough, but explore if there's product innovation involvement
- Technical writing → depends on context; API documentation at a tech company is very different from writing user manuals
- QA/testing → automation engineers have a strong case; manual testers need other evidence to lean on

### Hardest to Position
These roles face the steepest climb — but always ask about side projects, open source, or community work that could form the basis of an application instead:
- Non-tech industries using technology (e.g., finance person who uses Excel)
- Teaching technology at school level
- IT sales / account management
- Tech recruitment

## Borderline Cases — How to Assess

### The Outsourcing Question
The outsourcing/consulting work itself (at firms like TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Cognizant, etc.) won't form the basis of a GTV application — no successful endorsements have been observed from this work alone. However, that doesn't mean someone at these firms can't qualify. The path forward is through work **outside** the outsourcing role:

- Significant open source contributions with real adoption
- Side projects or startups with measurable traction
- Community contributions (speaking, mentoring, meetup organizing)
- Published work or research in their domain

If someone at an outsourcing firm has strong activity outside their day job, explore that as the foundation of their application. The outsourcing employment becomes background context, not the core of their case.

### The "Tech-Adjacent" Question
People in non-tech companies doing tech work — the question is how central tech is to their role:
- **Stronger positions**: Head of Engineering at a fintech; data science lead at a healthcare startup; built the tech platform for a retail company
- **Needs more work**: Manages the IT department at a bank; runs the website for a retail chain; does data entry automation — these can still work if the applicant has strong evidence of innovation or community contributions outside the day job

### The Freelancer/Contractor Question
Independent freelancers and contractors doing technical work:
- **Stronger**: Built their own products with users, freelance work resulted in shipped products they had ownership over, created tools/libraries adopted beyond their clients, have a portfolio of product-led work
- **Needs more work**: Pure service delivery (built what clients asked for), no products with independent traction, work is invisible once delivered — look for side projects, open source, or community work to strengthen the case
- Key: freelancing itself isn't disqualifying, but the evidence needs to show innovation and impact, not just billable hours

### The Hybrid Role Question
"Designer who codes," "creative technologist," "design engineer" — hybrid roles can qualify if:
- They are building digital products (not just designing marketing materials)
- The work involves product design + development, not just graphic/brand design
- They can demonstrate technical innovation alongside design work
- Assess based on what they actually build, not just their title

### The Seniority Question
Junior developers with fewer than 2-3 years of experience need evidence of outsized impact relative to career length. A standard junior dev career path alone — even at a great company — typically isn't enough, but there's a clear path forward:
- **Strategic pathway for juniors**: A thin CV can become a credible Promise application in 6-12 months with deliberate effort: contribute to open source projects with real adoption, speak at meetups and work toward conference CFPs, publish on editorially reviewed platforms, build a side project with measurable traction (users, revenue, GitHub stars). These activities simultaneously build OC3 evidence and strengthen the mandatory criterion

**Note**: The outsourcing question is covered in the Borderline Cases section above. While the outsourcing work itself won't qualify, always explore whether the applicant has qualifying work outside their primary role.

## Industry Context Signals

When assessing whether someone is in "digital technology," look for:
- Does their company build a digital product/platform?
- Is their work creating new technology, not just using existing technology?
- Would their achievements be recognized at a major tech conference?
- Could they credibly present at events like Web Summit, CogX, London Tech Week?
- Is their LinkedIn profile recognizable as a "tech person" to a Tech Nation assessor?

## Proof of Connection to Technology Business

If the applicant has been a founder or senior executive of a technology business in the last 5 years, they must provide evidence to prove this connection. Evidence can include:
- Domestic or international sales showing customer numbers for each country the company operates in
- Last set of audited accounts, signed by the directors, including balance sheet and profit and loss statement
- If no audited accounts, accounts must cover a period of more than 12 months

This is a separate requirement on top of the evidence documents — don't confuse it with the 10-document cap.

## Geographic Considerations

- Applicants can be from anywhere in the world
- UK-based applicants can also apply (switching from another visa, or extending)
- **UK-inside bias**: UK-based applicants appear to face additional scrutiny. To address this, be extra explicit about ecosystem contributions beyond your current employer, ensure recommenders include people external to your UK company, and emphasize how your work has international dimensions. This is a known challenge but not a blocker — many UK-based applicants succeed
- Remote work for a UK company doesn't require GTV, but GTV gives freedom to switch employers
- Tech Nation looks favorably on applicants who can articulate specific plans for the UK tech ecosystem
