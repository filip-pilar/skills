## Application Package

Since August 2025, applicants submit a single form via GOV.UK. The Home Office forwards the application to Tech Nation (the endorsing body) for assessment.

| Document | Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Personal statement | Part of GOV.UK form | Describes who you are, your achievements, why the UK, future plans |
| CV | Up to 3 sides of A4, typed | Must include career and any relevant publication history |
| Recommendation letters | 3 letters, up to 3 sides of A4 each (excluding author's credentials and contact details) | See detailed requirements below |
| Evidence documents | Up to 3 sides of A4 each, max 10 total | At least 2 for mandatory criterion + at least 4 for optional criteria (2 per selected criterion) |
| Proof of tech business connection | If founder/senior exec in last 5 years | Sales data, audited accounts, or accounts covering 12+ months |

**Key evidence rules:**
- CV and recommendation letters do NOT count toward the 10-document cap
- Each evidence document is assigned to one criterion only — but the same event or achievement can generate multiple different documents for different criteria. For example, a conference talk can become one document about community contribution (OC3) and a separate document about recognition (mandatory criterion), each with different framing, content, and proof. This is a powerful strategy for applicants with fewer distinct achievements
- Minimum evidence: 2 (mandatory) + 4 (optional) = 6 documents
- Maximum evidence: 10 documents total

## Exceptional Talent Criteria

### Mandatory Criterion (must satisfy)
Recognised by others as being a leading talent in the digital technology field in the last 5 years. (5 years is preferred but older achievements aren't automatically excluded, especially if they demonstrate trajectory — e.g., a well-known open source project from 6+ years ago still in active use, or early career work that clearly set the foundation for current impact.)

**What "recognised" means in practice:**
- External validation beyond your employer (press, awards, speaking invitations, industry recognition)
- Impact visible outside your immediate team/company
- Peers in the industry would know your name or your work
- The "it factor" / "X factor" — assessors are pattern-matching for "would this person stand out?" Similar signals to what YC or top accelerators look for
- Strong signals: previous exit, US VC backing, acceptance to recognised accelerators (YC, Entrepreneur First, Creative Destruction Lab), starting an AI company, former Google/DeepMind, Oxford PhD
- Weakest endorsed case on record: no media, no press — just pure pedigree from Entrepreneur First acceptance
- These signals can be codified, but ultimately assessors are asking: "would this person stand out in a room of tech leaders?"
- **Positioning tip**: If external recognition is thin, look for recognition you haven't framed as such — were you headhunted for your current role? Invited to a selective programme or accelerator? Sought out by a journalist, investor, or government body? These are signals of recognition even if they don't feel like awards

### Optional Criteria (must satisfy at least 2 of 5)

**OC1: Record of innovation as a founder or senior executive of a product-led digital technology company**
- Founded or co-founded a product-led tech company, OR held C-suite/VP/senior exec role
- Company must be product-led (not services/consulting/outsourcing)
- Evidence: funding raised, revenue, user growth, product launches, market impact
- Key: "product-led" is critical — the company must have built and shipped a digital product
- If claiming this, you must also provide proof of your connection to the technology business (sales data, audited accounts)
- Company size is irrelevant — a 2-person team can qualify if outcomes are demonstrable
- Outcomes must be defined and citable in the public domain (revenue, traction, third-party mentions)
- Third-party citation is crucial — if someone outside your company has pointed at the result, that's what matters
- Series A/B with a growth story (e.g., 0 to 60 employees) is strong but not required
- A side project with real traction counts if the outcomes are concrete and verifiable
- Raising £250k+ in funding is a meaningful signal of innovation and market validation. Smaller raises can work if paired with strong product traction (users, revenue, partnerships)

**OC2: Record of innovation where you've worked in a new digital technology field or concept as an employee**
- Innovation in an emerging or new area of digital technology
- Must show you worked on something genuinely new, not just used existing tech
- Evidence: novel technical approaches, new product categories, pioneering work in emerging fields
- The emphasis is on "new" — standard software engineering in established fields may not qualify
- "New" means frontier technology: AI, blockchain, deep tech, biotech, DNA/genome sequencing
- SaaS is borderline — standard SaaS work alone may not satisfy this criterion
- Works particularly well for PhDs with research in genuinely novel areas
- Example: a PhD student who built an open source lip-syncing tool as their Oxford thesis, accumulated significant GitHub stars, and got into YC — strong OC2 case
- Key differentiator from OC4: OC2 is about working in a genuinely new/frontier field; OC4 is about contributions in any product-led company

**OC3: Contributions to the digital technology sector outside of work**
- Mentoring other technologists
- Collaborative projects beyond your employment
- Open source contributions with significant adoption
- Community building (meetups, educational content with real reach)
- Contributions to technical standards bodies
- Evidence: GitHub stats, mentee outcomes, community metrics, standards contributions
- Formula: prestige of the event/platform x number of people touched x how hard it is to get in
- Government website citations carry significant weight
- GitHub stars: no minimum or maximum threshold — "quite arbitrary." Don't fixate on a number; focus on the story around the contribution
- Speaking: a talk at a prestigious, hard-to-get-into conference matters more than multiple talks at easy-access meetups
- Community building: assessed holistically — reach, effort, and recognition matter more than any single metric
- **Building OC3 evidence strategically**: If OC3 evidence is thin, these activities can be built over 3-6 months: propose conference talks (start with meetups and work up to larger events), contribute meaningfully to open source projects in your domain, start mentoring through a structured programme with a selection process, organize a meetup or community group, judge hackathons. The key is genuine sustained activity — a credible OC3 track record can be built, but it takes consistency over months, not a single burst

**OC4: Technical, commercial, or entrepreneurial contributions in a product-led digital technology company**
- Must have set up or worked in a product-led digital technology company
- Show YOUR specific contribution, not just the company's success
- Evidence: product impact you drove, revenue you influenced, technical systems you built, teams you grew
- This is the most common criterion for employees — but also the hardest to make compelling
- The company must be genuinely product-led (not services/consulting)
- Must be specific, clear, tangible, and show a meaningful change in the trajectory of the company
- Gold standard: being THE employee who drove something meaningful (not part of a large team effort)
- Rejections happen when the contribution isn't anchored strongly enough to the individual
- Use this criterion sparingly — only when the contribution is deep and clearly attributable to you
- Specificity is everything: "I contributed to growth" won't land. Frame as: "I built X, which caused Y, resulting in Z" — the more concrete, the stronger

**OC5: Research published or endorsed by an expert**
- Published research in peer-reviewed venues
- Research endorsed by a recognised expert in the field
- PhD or postdoctoral work with industry relevance
- Evidence: publications, citations, invited talks at academic conferences, expert endorsement letters
- PhD applications are strongest with publications, and applying without any is risky. If you don't have publications yet, the path forward is clear: workshop papers and arXiv preprints count, and conference posters and invited talks can partially compensate, especially when combined with other evidence of research impact. Consider publishing before applying — even one paper changes the picture significantly
- Publications can be in smaller journals; they don't all need to be top-tier venues
- Must be citable on Google Scholar
- If applying primarily on PhD pedigree, must provide a link to Google Scholar profile
- Publications are the figure of merit for this domain. Without them, assessors have no way to evaluate research impact

## Exceptional Promise Criteria

### Mandatory Criterion
Recognised by others as being a potential talent in the digital technology field in the last 5 years.

**Key difference from Talent**: Assessors look for trajectory and potential, not established track record. Early achievements that suggest where you're heading. Note the wording is "potential talent" — not "potential leading talent."

### Optional Criteria (must satisfy at least 2 of 5)
Same five categories as Talent, but evaluated against career stage:
1. Innovation as a founder of a product-led digital technology company (earlier stage OK, note: "senior executive" not mentioned for Promise)
2. Innovation in a new digital technology field or concept as an employee
3. Contributions to the digital technology sector outside of work (mentoring, collaborative projects)
4. Technical, commercial, or entrepreneurial contributions in a product-led digital technology company
5. Research published or endorsed by an expert

**Exceptional Talent bar**: 5+ years of full-time work experience in the field, with substance and demonstrable impact. The bar is an established track record with external recognition.

**Exceptional Promise bar**: Under 5 years, significantly more flexible. Could be a college grad who did a startup, got into Entrepreneur First, or has a combination of "it factor" signals backed by evidence (news, media mentions, early traction).

Real example: an applicant had mentions of an exit but not enough time in the field to qualify for Talent — defaulted to Promise, was initially rejected, reapplied and eventually succeeded.

Key distinction: Promise applicants don't need the depth of track record, but they do need to show trajectory and potential through concrete early signals.

## How the Endorsing Body Assesses Applications

Beyond checking criteria, the endorsing body also considers:
- Your career history — the international reputation and impact of your work
- The strength of the statements in your recommendation letters and of the evidence
- How you'll contribute to UK digital technology by being based in the UK
- Personal factors such as academic track record, salary level, company being hired by, or amount of money raised (if entrepreneur)
- The commercial impact of your previous work, achievements and experiences

## Evidence Document Structure (Jason's Template)

Each evidence document should follow this structure:
1. **Headline** — Tweet-style summary of the point this evidence makes
2. **Documents and proof** — Screenshots, letters, data, metrics
3. **Explanation** — How the proof supports the headline and connects to the criterion
4. **Call to action** — Why this matters, what it demonstrates about you

**Critical rule**: Each evidence document can only be used for ONE criterion. However, the same underlying event or achievement can generate separate documents for different criteria — each must have different framing, content, and proof. For example, a conference talk could produce one document about community contribution (OC3) and a separate document about recognition (mandatory criterion), using different angles and evidence in each.

**Example headlines:**
- Weak: "My contributions to open source software"
- Strong: "Built open-source lip-sync tool adopted by 5,000+ developers and cited by BBC News"
- Weak: "Leadership in AI research"
- Strong: "Led 3-person team that published first UK framework for algorithmic bias detection, adopted by 2 FTSE 100 companies"

## Recommendation Letter Requirements

**Format requirements (from GOV.UK):**
- 3 letters required
- Up to 3 sides of A4 each, **excluding the author's credentials and contact details**
- Must be typed and dated
- Signed by the author, or by someone on behalf of the organisation
- Must include: telephone number, email address, and the organisation's logo and registered address (if applicable)
- Must come with the author's CV or other proof of credentials that the endorsing body will accept

**Recommender qualifications:**
- Must be an "established expert" in digital technology (judged by the endorsing body)
- Must have known the applicant's work for at least 12 months

**Content requirements (each letter must):**
- Say how the recommender knows the applicant
- Show the applicant's achievements in digital technology and how they are a leader or potential leader
- Say how the applicant would benefit from living in the UK
- Say how the applicant would contribute to the digital technology field in the UK
- Explain what plans the applicant has for work in the future
- Be written specifically for this Global Talent application (cannot reuse a letter written for another purpose)
- Provide different examples from the other letters

**Strategic considerations:**
- Strong: industry leaders, conference organizers, senior figures at other companies who've used your work
- Need careful positioning: direct managers and colleagues (perceived bias — have them focus on specific witnessed achievements), friends or social contacts (very hard to use credibly), people without industry standing. If your only strong option is a manager, it can work with honest framing and detail-specific content — pair with an external recommender
- Usually the applicant prepares substance for the recommender to review and sign (common practice). The assistant must not draft or rewrite letter text; only provide planning structure, evidence mapping, and review questions
- Each letter should reference specific evidence documents where possible

## Common Rejection Patterns

- Insufficient external recognition (achievements only visible within own company) → **how to address**: seek speaking opportunities, publish in industry outlets, get quoted in press, accept podcast invitations
- Evidence doesn't clearly map to specific criteria → **how to address**: use the headline of each evidence document to explicitly state which criterion it supports and why — don't make the assessor guess
- Same evidence used for multiple criteria (not allowed)
- Inconsistent dates across documents
- Recommenders lack industry standing or can only speak generally → **how to address**: look beyond your immediate network — conference organizers, investors, executives at companies using your work, open source community leaders
- Recommenders haven't known applicant's work for 12+ months
- Tech consulting/outsourcing work without clear product innovation
- AI-generated content detected
- Personal statement too generic, doesn't tell a specific story
- Evidence documents lack external/third-party validation
- **Pathway mismatch**: applying for Exceptional Talent with under 5 years of experience. If your career length is under 5 years, Promise is the right pathway even if your achievements are strong — and Promise gives you the same day-to-day rights
- **UK-inside bias**: applications from people already in the UK appear to face additional scrutiny. To mitigate: (1) be extra explicit about ecosystem contribution and future plans beyond your current employer, (2) ensure at least one recommender is external to your current UK company, (3) emphasize international dimensions of your work, (4) make sure evidence shows impact beyond your current role. This is a risk factor but not a barrier — many UK-based applicants succeed
- **Lack of concrete metrics**: vague descriptions without specific numbers. If you've built a company, you need concrete results — revenue, users, growth rate, partnerships. "Scaled up" isn't enough, but the metrics don't need to be huge; they need to be specific and verifiable
- **Not specific enough**: vague descriptions of what you did without demonstrating clear, attributable impact → **how to address**: for every claim, ask "what specifically did I do, and what specifically happened as a result?" Then find proof of that specific result
