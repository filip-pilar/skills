## Application Package Overview

Since August 2025, all documents are submitted through a single GOV.UK form. The Home Office forwards the application to Tech Nation for assessment.

| Document | Limit | Count | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal statement | Part of GOV.UK form | 1 | Describes who you are, achievements, why UK, future plans |
| CV | Up to 3 sides of A4, typed | 1 | Must include career and publication history |
| Recommendation letters | Up to 3 sides of A4 each (excl. author credentials/contact details) | 3 | Each must come with author's CV |
| Evidence documents | Up to 3 sides of A4 each | Min 6, max 10 | At least 2 for mandatory + at least 4 for optional (2 per criterion) |
| Proof of tech business connection | As needed | If applicable | Required for founders/senior execs in last 5 years |

**Critical evidence rules:**
- CV and recommendation letters do NOT count toward the 10-document cap
- You cannot use the same evidence document for more than one criterion — but the same event or achievement can be the basis for multiple different documents, each framed for a different criterion with different content and proof
- Minimum: 2 (mandatory recognition) + 4 (optional, 2 per selected criterion) = 6 documents
- Maximum: 10 documents total

## Personal Statement

### Requirements
- Part of the GOV.UK application form (not a separate uploaded document)
- Written as if addressing a government official — who you are, what you've done, why the UK, what you'll contribute
- Must be in your own words (AI-generated content strictly prohibited)

### Narrative Structure
The personal statement tells your story. It should flow naturally while hitting these key beats:

1. **Who you are and your journey** — How you entered digital tech, what drives you
2. **What you've achieved** — Key innovations, contributions, impact (with specific metrics)
3. **External recognition** — How the industry recognizes your work
4. **Why the UK** — Specific plans, what you'll contribute to the UK tech ecosystem

**GOV.UK form limit: 7,000 characters** (~700 words, ~2.5 pages) — confirmed as of March 2026.

Think of it like a Stanford admission essay, not a startup pitch. Tell the personal story — the sequence of events that led you to this point. This is about YOU, not your current startup. Showcase personality.

Common mistake: focusing too heavily on the immediate company you're building. The assessor wants to understand the person, not the pitch. The personal statement is your outward view of the world — make it feel human and genuine.

### What Makes It Compelling
- Specific numbers and metrics, not vague claims
- A clear thread connecting your work to broader impact
- Authentic voice — assessors read hundreds of these, genuine writing stands out
- Forward-looking UK plans that are specific and credible
- References to evidence documents ("as shown in Evidence Document 3...")

### UK Plans Section

The GOV.UK form asks: **"How will the UK digital technology sector benefit from your work?"** This section is actively evaluated and is a common differentiator between endorsed and refused applications.

**What assessors want to see:**
- **Activities beyond your day job** — the guidance explicitly asks about "activities you will take part in outside of your direct occupation." Mentoring, community building, open source, speaking — not just employment plans
- **A specific city or region and why** — the guidance asks where you plan to live. Connect the location to your work (e.g., London for fintech/investor access, Cambridge for deep tech/university collaboration, Edinburgh for AI research)
- **Plans that logically extend your track record** — if you've never mentored anyone, claiming you'll become a prolific mentor isn't credible. Plans must connect to things you already demonstrably do
- **Evidence you've already started** — attending UK events remotely, connecting with UK communities, conversations with UK companies or accelerators. Concrete steps taken signal genuine intent

**UK tech ecosystem elements to reference (pick what's relevant to you):**
- **Research institutions**: Alan Turing Institute (data science/AI), Catapult Centres (Digital Catapult, Connected Places Catapult), university partnerships (Imperial, UCL, Oxford, Cambridge, Edinburgh)
- **Funding/support**: Innovate UK, UKRI grants, British Business Bank, FCA Regulatory Sandbox (fintech)
- **Accelerators**: Entrepreneur First, Techstars London, Seedcamp, Founders Factory, Bethnal Green Ventures (tech for good)
- **Events/communities**: London Tech Week, CogX, tech meetup groups, Knowledge Transfer Networks, BCS
- **Government priorities**: UK AI Strategy, AI Safety Institute, National Data Strategy, R&D tax credits

**Common mistakes:**
- Generic praise: "The UK is a great place for tech" with no connection to your specific expertise
- Plans that could apply to any country — nothing UK-specific
- Omitting a city/region entirely
- Only discussing employment, not ecosystem contribution
- Wasting character count on emotional backstory instead of concrete plans
- Making claims that don't match your track record

**Strong structure**:
- City or region
- Specific ecosystem reason tied to the applicant's expertise
- Professional plan
- Outside-work contribution
- Concrete UK-facing step already taken

**Weak structure**:
- Generic praise for the UK
- Broad collaboration or innovation claims
- No city, no named ecosystem fit, no concrete plan, no connection to the applicant's track record

## CV

### Requirements
- Up to 3 sides of A4, typed
- Must include career and any relevant publication history
- Dates must EXACTLY match LinkedIn profile, personal statement, and Home Office form
- Focus on impact, not responsibilities

### Structure for Tech Nation
Unlike a job-hunting CV, this CV should emphasize:
- **Key achievements section** at the top — your 5 strongest accomplishments with metrics
- **Professional experience** — impact-focused bullet points, not job descriptions
- **Products shipped** — what you built, your role, measurable impact
- **Open source** — projects, role, engagement metrics
- **Speaking & publications** — conferences, articles, podcasts
- **Awards & recognition**

The CV is the "least important" document in the application — don't over-optimise.

Key difference from a standard job-hunting CV: under each experience item, explain WHY it matters to the UK ("I built X. This matters because Y for the UK tech ecosystem"). This serves two purposes: (1) shows you've done your homework understanding the UK, (2) shows you understand what makes your experience relevant.

Dates must EXACTLY match LinkedIn — assessors actually verify this. Think of it like a job application: tailored to the "employer" (the UK government).

### Date Consistency
This is critical. Create a date checklist:
- Every role: exact start month/year and end month/year
- These must match across CV, personal statement, LinkedIn, and Home Office form
- Any gaps must be explainable
- Company names must be consistent (e.g., don't say "Google" in one place and "Alphabet" in another)

## Recommendation Letters

### Requirements (from GOV.UK)
- 3 letters required
- Up to 3 single sides of A4 paper, **excluding the author's credentials and contact details**
- Must be typed and dated
- Signed by the author, or by someone on behalf of the organisation recommending you
- Must include: telephone number, email address, and the organisation's logo and registered address (if applicable)
- Must come with the author's CV (or other proof of their credentials that the endorsing body will accept)

### Recommender Qualifications
- Must be an **"established expert"** in digital technology (the endorsing body judges this)
- Must have known the applicant's work for **at least 12 months**
- Each letter must be written specifically for this Global Talent application — cannot reuse letters written for other purposes

### Content Requirements (each letter must)
- Say how the recommender knows the applicant
- Show the applicant's achievements in the digital technology sector, and how they are a leader or potential leader
- Say how the applicant would benefit from living in the UK
- Say how the applicant would contribute to the digital technology field in the UK
- Explain what plans the applicant has for work in the future
- Provide **different examples** from the other letters

### Who Should Write Them
Choose recommenders by evidential value, not relationship convenience. The best letter writer is usually the credible person who can verify the applicant's strongest recent evidence in concrete terms.

**Strong recommenders:**
- Industry leaders at recognized organizations
- Senior figures who've seen your work's impact
- Conference organizers who invited you to speak
- Open source community leaders who know your contributions
- CTOs/founders at companies that used your product/work
- People with their own industry credibility

**Recommenders who need careful positioning:**
- Direct managers or colleagues — perceived bias, but can work if they focus on specific achievements they witnessed and the relationship is stated honestly. Pair with at least one external recommender to balance
- Friends or social contacts — very hard to use credibly; look for a professional angle instead
- People who can only speak generally — coach them to be specific, or find someone who knows the work more closely
- Anyone without standing in digital technology — check if they have credentials you haven't considered (advisory roles, board positions, publications)
- Anyone who has known the applicant's work for less than 12 months — this is a hard requirement from gov.uk
- Old managers or colleagues whose main knowledge is dated — use only if they can verify a major achievement that remains central to the application. Recent senior witnesses to stronger evidence should usually beat them.

### Selection Rule
Use the detailed ranking rubric in `interview-guide.md`. The short version: 12+ months knowledge is mandatory; direct evidence fit beats seniority alone; recent credible witnesses usually beat easy old contacts; the final 3 should balance internal and external perspectives.

### Strategic Pairing
- Each recommender should reference different evidence documents where possible
- Together, the 3 letters should cover all target criteria
- Different recommenders can speak to different aspects of the applicant's work
- Each letter must provide DIFFERENT examples (gov.uk requirement)
- Usually the applicant prepares bullet-point substance for the recommender to review and sign. The assistant must not draft or rewrite letter text
- The final 3 should be explicitly stronger than backups. Record why each selected recommender was chosen and why close alternates were not.

### How to Approach Recommenders

**People you know well**: Brief message explaining you're applying for GTV, why you'd value their letter specifically, and that you'll draft bullet points for them to write from. Most people say yes when the burden is clear and low.

**People you know professionally but not closely** (conference organizers, investors, executives at companies using your work): Lead with specific shared context ("You invited me to speak at X" / "Your company has been using our API since 2024"). Explain the GTV process briefly. Offer to draft bullet points.

**Looking beyond your immediate network**: The 12-month knowledge requirement means you need someone who genuinely knows your work. Think about who has observed your work indirectly — someone who reviewed your conference talk proposal, an open source maintainer who merged your PRs, a judge at a hackathon you won.

## Proof of Connection to Technology Business

If the applicant has been a founder or senior executive of a technology business in the last 5 years, they MUST provide:
- Domestic or international sales showing customer numbers for each country the company operates in
- Their last set of audited accounts, signed by the directors, including balance sheet and profit and loss statement
- If no audited accounts, accounts covering a period of more than 12 months

This is a separate requirement — it does not count toward the 10-document evidence cap.

## Evidence Documents

### Requirements
- Mandatory criterion: at least 2 documents (proving recognition as leading/potential talent)
- Optional criteria: at least 2 documents per selected criterion (so at least 4 total for the 2 required criteria)
- Total: minimum 6, maximum 10 documents
- Each document: up to 3 sides of A4
- **Each document is assigned to one criterion only** — but the same event can generate different documents for different criteria, each with distinct framing and proof

### Evidence Document Template (Jason's Structure)
1. **Headline** — Tweet-style summary (one sentence capturing the point)
2. **Documents and proof** — Screenshots, letters, data, metrics (visual evidence)
3. **Explanation** — How the proof supports the headline and connects to the criterion
4. **Call to action** — Why this matters, what it demonstrates

### What Makes Evidence Strong
- **Specificity**: Exact numbers, dates, names — not vague claims
- **Third-party validation**: Press coverage, awards, external metrics (not self-reported)
- **Measurable impact**: Users, revenue, downloads, citations — quantifiable results
- **External recognition**: Evidence that people outside your company value your work
- **Clear criterion mapping**: Reader immediately understands which criterion this supports
- **Quality over quantity**: 6 strong pieces beat 10 weak ones every time. Use fewer documents if each is compelling
- **Recency**: All evidence should ideally be from the last 5 years. Evidence created very close to submission date can look manufactured if it's isolated — but regular sustained activity over months is convincing. If building new evidence, start early and maintain consistency so that it reads as genuine ongoing engagement, not last-minute padding
- **Verifiability**: Every claim should be independently verifiable. Include public URLs, press articles, or named contacts who can confirm

### What Makes Evidence Weak
- Vague claims without proof ("led a team" without showing what the team achieved)
- Internal company metrics only (no external validation)
- Screenshots of dashboards without context
- Evidence that shows activity but not impact
- Documents that could apply to anyone in a similar role
- **Self-published content** (LinkedIn posts, personal blog, unreviewed Medium articles) does not count as meaningful recognition — the platform must have an editorial review process
- **Sponsored or paid speaking** does not count as independent recognition — the event must have an independent selection process
- **Evidence that can't be verified online** — assessors check. If they can't find it, it doesn't count

### Formatting Requirements
- Submit as PDF (not Word, not JPG)
- Use descriptive file names: "Evidence_1_Speaking_at_conferences.pdf" not "doc1.pdf"
- Total attachments must not exceed 25MB
- Open each document with a 5-6 sentence paragraph explaining what this evidence is, which criterion it supports, and why it matters
- Highlight your name wherever it appears in screenshots
- Include links to external resources (URLs, GitHub repos, videos) as backup — assessors may not check them but they demonstrate depth

### Criterion-Specific Evidence Notes

**OC1 (founder/exec)**: Should demonstrate real market traction — revenue, paying customers, user growth. A founder with awards but no revenue and no funding will be rejected. Audited or director-signed financials covering 12+ months showing revenue beyond the founder's salary.

**OC2 (innovation in new field)**: Should relate to product-led tech. Agency, consulting, or outsourced project work does not qualify. Include employer confirmation letter (from someone different from the 3 main recommenders), product screenshots, and before/after impact metrics.

**OC3 (outside of work)**: All activities must be voluntary and not employer-sponsored. Mentoring should be through structured programmes with a selection process — generic online mentoring platforms may not be sufficient. Speaking must be at events with an independent selection process, not company-sponsored slots. Regular activity over years beats a recent burst.

**OC4 (contributions in product-led company)**: Quantify: revenue generated, downloads, funding raised, team growth. Salary, equity, and bonuses are reported to no longer be valid under OC4 (2025 guideline update) — use these under the mandatory criterion instead.

**OC5 (research)**: Peer-reviewed papers with citation metrics and journal impact factors. Patents with full identifying reference. Must be endorsed by recognised experts, not self-published.

### Building Evidence Before Applying

If criteria evidence is thin, some of the strongest evidence can be built strategically before submitting. This is not about manufacturing credentials — it's about doing real work that demonstrates what you're capable of. Activities must be genuine and sustained; start 3-6 months before your planned submission date.

**OC3 (contributions outside work)** — easiest to build:
- Speak at conferences: start with local meetups and lightning talks, then submit CFPs to larger events. Even a single talk at a well-regarded conference is strong
- Contribute to open source: meaningful contributions to established projects in your domain (not trivial PRs). Maintain your own project if it gains real adoption
- Mentor through structured programmes: ADPList, Codebar, Google Summer of Code mentoring, coding bootcamp mentoring. Programmes with selection processes are stronger than informal mentoring
- Judge hackathons or review grants: shows the community trusts your expertise
- Organize meetups: starting or consistently running a tech meetup shows sustained community leadership

**Mandatory criterion (recognition)** — takes longer but highly impactful:
- Get quoted in press: reach out to journalists covering your domain, offer expert commentary
- Publish on editorially reviewed platforms: guest posts on well-known industry blogs, magazine articles, not self-published content
- Accept podcast invitations or seek them out in your niche
- Speak at industry events where you're introduced as an expert

**OC1 (founder/exec innovation)** — if close to a threshold:
- Crossing £250k in funding raised adds significant weight to a founder application
- Securing named customers, partnerships, or accelerator acceptance provides third-party validation

**General principles:**
- Quality over quantity — one meaningful conference talk beats five casual meetup appearances
- Build a trail: each activity should be independently verifiable (event websites, recordings, GitHub history)
- Consistency matters — monthly activity over 4 months is far more convincing than everything in one week

Good evidence is succinct — you can prove a point in one page, in a few lines.

Example: remuneration evidence = one screenshot of contract + pay slip + bank statement. Done. Everything can be boiled down to less than 3 pages.

Strong evidence hooks you in the first two lines — if the opening doesn't land, the rest won't save it. People who are really good at evidence documents don't use all 3 pages — brevity signals confidence.

The hook matters most: lead with the most impressive fact, not the backstory.

### Evidence Mapping Strategy
Map evidence to criteria before writing:

| Evidence Item | Target Criterion | Type (Mandatory/Optional) |
|---|---|---|
| [item] | [criterion] | [M/O] |

Ensure:
- Each target criterion has at least 2 evidence items
- Mandatory criterion has your strongest evidence
- No evidence document is assigned to more than one criterion (same event can generate separate documents for different criteria)
- Evidence items don't repeat the same point

### What Counts as Evidence (from GOV.UK)
Examples of evidence that could support eligibility:
- Starting or contributing to open source projects in a way that peers have acknowledged has advanced the field
- Relevant qualifications, or documentation of speaking at a prestigious conference
- A prize granted by an organisation of a similar standing and distinction to the Royal Society in the UK
